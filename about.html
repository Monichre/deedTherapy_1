Mark's Story

Mark Wildermann has over twenty years of experience in the educational field (mostly at the college level in both student development and academic departments) and is well grounded in life experience. At the age of seventeen, he dropped out of college after three days to work on a freighter; returning to school he was baffled in his freshman year by the introductory psychology course which presented behaviorism as explanation of the human being; several years later in Switzerland he visited the Bollingen Tower residence of the founder of analytical psychology Carl Jung; after graduating with a B.A. in English he wrote plays and screenplays while working as a baker and truck driver among other things. He earned an M.Ed. in Mental Health which is balanced by training and practice in several alternative healing modalities: Transcendental Meditation, Anthroposophy, Reiki Master Teacher, Past Life Regression Therapist, Bach Flower Therapist, and Nationally Certified Hypnotherapist. He has worked with the most diverse individuals and groups at all stages of personal development. The unceasing rewards and challenges of those relationships have taught him to adapt techniques and treatments for the unique situation that’s always presented. No stone left unturned. No client left unrecognized.

Philosophy

A diagnosis can be accepted, unfortunately, as an identity rather than a description of one’s moods and thoughts. In perceiving the world, the soul naturally shifts states as it struggles always for balance and harmony.

"When a dog runs at you, whistle for him."
- Henry David Thoreau

One’s temperament, which is formed through both inherited characteristics and environmental influences, will respond accordingly. Our purpose is to apprehend and learn through this window of perception as life teaches us through challenges, indeed through suffering, to evolve and develop. Darwinian evolutionary theory ought to be understood as a process. Within the present Western culture, specifically the American model of individual freedom and responsibility, it should only be natural and commonsensical that the responsibility of this modern self-consciousness is to adapt and evolve one’s own being. We are formed with three modes of encountering both inner and outer phenomena: thinking, feeling, and willing. Just as we assume we can work on the physical body – muscle and other conditioning through exercise and diet – so also we can nurture and strengthen our thinking, feeling, and willing.
